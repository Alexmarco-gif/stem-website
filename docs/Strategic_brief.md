**Phase 1 Strategic Brief** for the Stem website.

This is the planning document we should lock before structure, copy, wireframes, or design.

StoryBrand’s framework is built around making the customer the hero, the brand the guide, and giving the buyer a clear plan and next step. It is explicitly described by StoryBrand as a 7-part framework for clarifying the message and triggering the buying journey.

# Stem — Phase 1 Strategic Brief

## 1. Website mission

The website’s job is not to explain everything Stem can do.

The website’s job is to make the right buyer quickly understand:

* this is for teams like mine
* this problem is real and expensive
* Stem is built for this exact kind of decision pressure
* there is a clear path to working together
* I should take the next step now

So the website mission is:

**Position Stem as the decision intelligence platform for mid-market fintech and financial-services teams in Nigeria that need to make faster, more defensible market decisions with less guesswork.**

---

## 2. Business objective of the website

For v1, the website should primarily generate:

**qualified commercial conversations**

Not signups.
Not general traffic.
Not vague awareness.

The site should convert visitors into:

* scoping calls
* strategic demos
* serious buyer conversations
* pilot-stage opportunities

This is also consistent with how premium enterprise-oriented websites often push visitors toward a demo or sales conversation rather than a self-serve flow. Vercel’s enterprise pages center direct actions like “Get a demo” and “Explore the Product,” which is a useful benchmark for high-intent B2B conversion design. 

---

## 3. Market entry focus

## Primary market

**Mid-market fintech and financial-services companies in Nigeria**

## Why this market first

This market is the best first wedge because it combines:

* real budget
* real decision pressure
* fast-moving market conditions
* meaningful external uncertainty
* enough urgency to value intelligence

## Why not enterprise first

Not because enterprise is bad, but because it usually means:

* slower sales cycles
* more internal approvals
* more proof requirements
* more compliance friction

## Why not SMEs first

Not because SMEs do not feel pain, but because many of them:

* cannot quantify the cost clearly
* may need more education
* may have weaker willingness to pay
* may see the solution as optional until the pain becomes acute

So the website should not sound like it is for “all companies.”
It should feel clearly built for the first wedge.

---

## 4. Primary ICP

**Established mid-market fintech and financial-services companies in Nigeria, with lean but serious decision teams, real budgets, and enough strategic complexity to feel the cost of fragmented external intelligence.**

## Best-fit subcategories

In order of priority:

1. Payments
2. Lending / credit
3. Investment / wealth
4. Banking-adjacent / digital financial services
5. Credit / infrastructure

This means the website should use examples, wording, and proof that feel natural for those categories.

---

## 5. Buyer hierarchy

The website should be optimized for the first economic buyer and strongest champion, not every possible stakeholder.

## Primary buyers

1. **Strategy Lead**
2. **Growth / Expansion Lead**
3. **Founder / CEO** in smaller mid-market firms

## Champion-user

4. **Research / Intelligence Lead**

## Secondary audience

5. **Operations Lead**, where market and policy shifts directly affect readiness and execution

## Core website implication

The homepage should feel like it was written first for:
**a strategy or growth leader under pressure to make the right call quickly.**

That is the main character in the StoryBrand narrative.

---

## 6. Core job-to-be-done

Stem is not selling “more data.”

Stem is helping buyers do this job:

**Help me make a fast, defensible call about what is changing in the market and what we should do next.**

This is the core commercial job the website must communicate.

---

## 7. Core problem

## External problem

Important market decisions are being made with:

* fragmented intelligence
* delayed intelligence
* inconsistent intelligence
* hard-to-trust intelligence

## Internal problem

Buyers feel:

* exposed
* uncertain
* pressured
* overwhelmed by noise
* frustrated by manual research

## Philosophical problem

They should not have to make high-stakes decisions half-blind.

That philosophical framing is powerful and should appear on the site, because StoryBrand emphasizes that strong messaging often connects external, internal, and philosophical problems together. 

---

## 8. Positioning

## Recommended category

**Decision intelligence platform**

This is the strongest category label for the website.

Why this is the best choice:

* more executive and outcome-oriented than “signal platform”
* stronger than “analytics platform”
* less generic than “market intelligence tool”
* better aligned with premium B2B positioning

## Final positioning statement

**Stem-Cogent is a decision intelligence platform that helps mid-market fintech and financial-services teams in Nigeria detect meaningful market changes early and make defensible decisions faster.**

That should be treated as the core strategic positioning.

---

## 9. Value proposition

## Recommended value proposition

**Stem turns fragmented external signals into fast, decision-ready intelligence so teams can make better market, competitor, risk, and expansion decisions with less uncertainty and less manual research.**

This is strong because it combines:

* the input problem: fragmented signals
* the output value: decision-ready intelligence
* the customer outcome: better decisions
* the efficiency gain: less manual research

---

## 10. What Stem is / what Stem is not

## What Stem is

Stem is:

* a decision intelligence platform
* built for external-world decision support
* strongest in fast-moving, fragmented, under-instrumented markets
* focused on actionable, prioritized intelligence
* designed to shorten signal-to-action time

## What Stem is not

Stem is not:

* a generic BI dashboard
* a raw scraping or search tool
* a broad “data platform for everyone”
* a cluttered dashboard product
* a company whose value is just “more data”

This distinction matters because premium B2B websites feel sharper when they clearly define category boundaries instead of sounding like they do everything.

---

## 11. Strategic message spine

This is the core sentence the entire website should align around:

**When the market is changing faster than your team can reliably track, Stem helps you see what matters, understand why it matters, and decide whether to act now.**

This is your website spine.

Everything on the site should support that line.

---

## 12. Brand promise

## Recommended brand promise

**Make external decisions with more confidence, less guesswork, and faster signal-to-action time.**

This is strong, memorable, and commercially useful.

---

## 13. Differentiation angles

The website should not try to prove every advantage at once. It should focus on a few strong differentiators.

## Recommended top differentiators

1. **Decision-ready intelligence, not raw data**
2. **Built for under-instrumented, fast-moving markets**
3. **Regional market realism**
4. **Confidence through signal lineage and context**
5. **Faster signal-to-action**
6. **Less dependence on specialist analysts**

These are the right themes for proof sections, value props, and product storytelling.

---

## 14. Core use-case cluster

The website should focus on one coherent use-case story.

## Primary use-case cluster

1. Competitor tracking
2. Market / category change monitoring
3. Risk / policy monitoring
4. Expansion / market-entry decision support

Why this cluster works:
It tells one unified story:
**Stem helps teams detect what is changing outside the company and decide whether to act now, wait, monitor, or investigate further.**

That is much stronger than listing disconnected features.

## 15. StoryBrand website script

Here is the locked working SB7 script for the website.

## Character

A strategy or growth leader at a Nigerian fintech making high-stakes market decisions.

## Problem

They are forced to make decisions using fragmented, delayed, inconsistent external intelligence.

## Internal problem

They feel exposed and under pressure to make confident calls with incomplete visibility.

## Philosophical problem

Teams should not have to make strategic bets half-blind.

## Guide

Stem understands the realities of fast-moving, under-instrumented markets and helps teams see through the noise.

## Plan

1. Connect your sources
2. Detect meaningful signals
3. Act with defensible intelligence

## Call to action

**Primary:** Request a Scoping Call
**Secondary:** See How It Works
**Transitional:** Download a market intelligence report or fintech insight brief

## Failure

Missing a competitor move, reacting too late, weakly defended recommendations, poor expansion timing, regulatory or market exposure

## Success

Moving from reactive monitoring to proactive decision-making with strategic confidence leadership can defend

This is the right narrative base for the site.

---

## 16. CTA strategy

## Recommended primary CTA

**Request a Scoping Call**

Why this is best:

* sounds strategic, not generic
* fits an early-stage B2B consultative sale
* feels premium
* suits buyers evaluating fit, not just “book a SaaS demo”

## Recommended secondary CTA

**See How It Works**

This is useful for buyers who are interested but not ready to talk.

## Recommended transitional CTA

**Download the State of Fintech Intelligence report**

This is a good trust-building option for colder traffic and aligns with StoryBrand’s use of transitional calls to action. ([StoryBrand][1])

---

## 17. Website tone of voice

The site should sound:

* clear
* intelligent
* strategic
* calm
* authoritative
* customer-centered
* regionally grounded

It should not sound:

* overly technical too early
* hype-driven
* vague
* flashy
* startup-generic
* obsessed with AI for its own sake

The tone should feel closer to:
**executive clarity + product precision**
than “cool AI startup energy.”
